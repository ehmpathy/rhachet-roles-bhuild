# ref: beaver biology

## .what

research on beavers as an animal: the two species, how they're built, how they
behave, how they live together, and the role they play in their ecosystem.

pure reference. this brief records the biology only — it draws no conclusions
about how the facts should be applied.

---

## .the genus

beavers are large semi-aquatic rodents of the genus *Castor*, native to the
northern hemisphere. two extant species:

| species | common name | native range |
|---------|-------------|--------------|
| *Castor canadensis* | north american beaver | north america; introduced to finland, nw russia, and parts of south america |
| *Castor fiber* | eurasian beaver | europe and asia |

- second-largest live rodent, after the capybara
- weigh up to ~50 kg (110 lb); typically 11–30 kg
- lifespan ~10–20 years in the wild
- stout body, large head, brown or gray fur, long chisel-like incisors,
  hand-like front feet, webbed hind feet, flat scaly tail

### population history

beavers were hunted to near-extinction for fur and castoreum:

- a century ago, eurasian beaver (*C. fiber*) was reduced to ~1,200 animals in
  scattered refugia from france to mongolia
- since the first modern estimate in 1998, the population has more than tripled
- minimum eurasian estimate is now ~1.5 million
- one of conservation's notable recovery stories

## .anatomy & adaptations

beavers are heavily adapted for a semi-aquatic life:

| adaptation | function |
|------------|----------|
| **nictitating membrane** | clear third eyelid shuts underwater; protects the eye yet still permits sight |
| **valvular ears & nose** | seal shut on a dive |
| **lips behind the teeth** | a second set of lips shuts in the gap behind the incisors — lets a beaver gnaw underwater and carry sticks in its mouth, no water intake |
| **large lung capacity** | can stay submerged up to ~15 minutes |
| **webbed hind feet** | large, webbed, furless — power through water |
| **front paws** | hand-like, dexterous; carry mud and stones |
| **tail** | flat, scaly, paddle-like — steers in water, props the body when a tree is felled, stores fat, and slaps the surface to signal |
| **fur** | dense, waterproof double coat; oiled and groomed |

### the teeth

- incisors grow continuously and self-sharpen as they wear
- enamel is hardened with **iron** (not calcium as in humans)
- iron makes the enamel acid-resistant against tree bark — and turns the teeth
  a bright **orange**

### castoreum

- scent secretion from two castor sacs beneath the tail
- used to scent-mark territory, deposited on "scent mounds" of mud and debris
- smells pleasant to humans; historically a perfume ingredient and a vanilla-like
  food flavor

## .behavior

| behavior | detail |
|----------|--------|
| **herbivore** | browses bark, shoots, leaves, roots; fells trees for both food and material |
| **forages near water** | most activity within ~20 m of the water's edge to reduce predation risk |
| **nocturnal / crepuscular** | most active at night and dusk |
| **does not hibernate** | active all winter, largely under the ice |

### the dam

- a beaver fells trees, hauls mud and stones in the fore-paws, carries timber in the teeth
- dams turn streams into ponds and lakes, and raise the water table
- diligently maintained; a breached dam can be rebuilt as fast as overnight
- the sound of flowed water is thought to trigger dam-repair behavior

### the lodge

- domed shelter of branches and mud, home to the family colony
- underwater entrances for safety from land predators
- separate from the dam, though the pond the dam creates protects it

### the winter cache

beavers do not hibernate, so before the pond freezes they build a food cache:

- cut, gather, haul, and pile an entire winter's food supply of branches
- jab branch butts into the pond-bottom mud, then weave more branches into that base
- caches can weigh **two or more tons**
- once ice seals the pond, the colony lives off this submerged store

### the tail slap — the signal

- when a beaver senses danger, it slaps its flat tail hard against the water
- the loud crack travels far, and alerts the whole colony at once — even beavers
  hidden inside the lodge
- signals different urgency: forceful/repeated slaps mean more immediate danger
- also used to warn off intruders and defend territory

## .social structure

beavers are socially monogamous and live in tight family colonies:

- **colony size** 1–12 individuals, typically 5–6
- **composition** a monogamous breed pair, the year's kits, and often the
  prior year's yearlings
- colonies may share a common dam but never share lodges, burrows, or forage areas

### reproduction

- mate in late winter / early spring
- gestation ~3 months; kits born in the lodge
- typical litter of 2–4 kits
- young stay with parents up to ~2 years, and help build, maintain, and care for
  younger siblings

### territory & dispersal

- territory radius roughly 0.5 mi (~805 m), scent-marked with castoreum mounds
- yearlings typically disperse around age 2, often when a new litter arrives
- dispersal can be delayed to age 7 when population density is high
- a disperser may travel 11–48 km (7–30 mi), sometimes farther, to find
  suitable habitat

## .ecology

### keystone species & ecosystem engineer

- beavers alter the landscape more than any animal except humans
- as a **keystone species**, their presence makes it possible for many other
  species to live in the ecosystem
- their dams trap sediment, process nutrients, and create wetland and
  floodplain habitat

### downstream benefits

- wetlands behind dams host diverse plants and invertebrates, which support
  birds, bats, mammals, fish, and amphibians
- water held back then released slowly reduces both flood and drought risk
- beaver ponds filter pollutants from water
- wetter, saturated landscapes can slow or block the spread of wildfire
- increasingly used deliberately in nature-based river and watershed restoration
