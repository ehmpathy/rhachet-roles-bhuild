
## the outcome world

### what does a day-in-the-life look like with this in place?

**before:**
a mechanic receives a bug report: "the invoice total is wrong". they trace it to a calculation function, add a +1 offset to fix the visible symptom, and ship. three weeks later, a similar bug appears in a different report — the real cause was a timezone conversion that happens upstream. the bandaid fix masked the real issue and created false confidence.

similarly for features: a mechanic builds a "retry button" because requests sometimes fail. they never ask _why_ requests fail or _who_ would use it. turns out it's a rate limit from a third-party api — the retry button makes it worse. and the actual users wanted visibility into failures, not a retry.

**after:**
before the blueprint is finalized, three reflection stones force deep questions:

1. **audience** — _who is this for?_
   - who will experience this change?
   - why do they care? how much?
   - how will they experience it?

2. **rootcause** — _why is this needed?_
   - 5 whys drill with branches
   - symptom vs cause vs root cause
   - recommended layer to address

3. **premortem** — _how could this fail?_
   - imagine we shipped and it failed — why?
   - what assumptions could be wrong?
   - what's the nightmare scenario?

the mechanic now builds with empathy (audience), depth (rootcause), and caution (premortem). the blueprint addresses the real problem for the real users, with eyes open to risks.

### what's the before/after contrast?

| aspect | before | after |
|--------|--------|-------|
| audience | implicit or assumed | explicit: who, why, how much |
| problem analysis | superficial — fix visible symptom | deep — drill to root cause with 5 whys |
| risk awareness | optimistic — assume it works | cautious — premortem surfaces failure modes |
| blueprint review | checks coverage and consistency | also checks: right audience? right problem? right risks? |
| rework rate | high — bandaids and surprises | low — durable fixes, known risks |

### what's the "aha" moment where the value clicks?

> "oh, we were so focused on the technical fix that we never asked who actually cares about this or what could go wrong. the reflection stones made us pause and think before we built."

the triad of audience + rootcause + premortem transforms reactive work into deliberate craft.

## user experience

### what usecases do folks fulfill? what goals?

1. **mechanic investigates a bug**: before they jump to code, they complete the reflection stones. this surfaces who cares, why it's really broken, and what could go wrong with the fix.

2. **mechanic designs a feature**: before blueprint, they reflect on who the audience is, why the feature is needed at the root, and how the feature could fail.

3. **reviewer validates a blueprint**: the self-review forces verification that reflections were done and the blueprint addresses them.

4. **wisher observes progress**: sees explicit audience analysis, root cause drill, and risk assessment — gains confidence the right problem is solved the right way.

### what contract inputs & outputs do they leverage?

**audience stone:**

| question | what it surfaces |
|----------|------------------|
| who is the audience? | persona, role, context |
| why do they care? | motivation, pain, goal |
| how much do they care? | priority, urgency, stakes |
| what do they care about? | specific aspects, criteria |
| how will they experience it? | touchpoints, journey, friction |

**rootcause stone:**

| output | description |
|--------|-------------|
| 5 whys tree | branches for alternative hypotheses |
| root cause(s) | identified cause(s) at the deepest layer |
| symptom vs cause | clear distinction |
| recommendation | which layer to address |

**premortem stone:**

| frame | question |
|-------|----------|
| premortem | imagine we shipped and it failed — why? |
| premortem | what obvious signs did we ignore? |
| premortem | what would make us regret this? |
| inversion | where don't we want to go, and how could we get there? |
| inversion | what assumptions could turn out wrong? |
| inversion | how would a critic attack this? |
| inversion | what's the nightmare scenario? |

**new self-review in blueprint guard (has-addressed-reflections)**

checks:
- does the blueprint address the audience identified?
- does the blueprint address the root cause identified?
- does the blueprint mitigate the risks identified?
- if any are skipped, is that explicitly justified?

**3.1.5.research.reflection.product.audience._.v1.i1.md:**

```markdown
## audience reflection

### who is the audience?
- **primary**: finance team who reconcile invoices daily
- **secondary**: customers who receive invoices via email
- **tertiary**: auditors who review invoice accuracy quarterly

### why do they care?
- finance team: incorrect totals cause reconciliation failures, wasted hours
- customers: trust erodes when invoices don't match expectations
- auditors: discrepancies trigger compliance reviews

### how much do they care?
- finance team: HIGH — blocks their daily workflow
- customers: MEDIUM — notice but may not complain immediately
- auditors: HIGH — compliance is non-negotiable

### what do they care about?
- accuracy to the cent
- consistency across invoice types
- clear breakdown of line items

### how will they experience it?
- finance team: totals match their spreadsheet calculations
- customers: invoice matches their purchase records
- auditors: no discrepancies in quarterly review
```

**3.1.5.research.reflection.product.rootcause._.v1.i1.md:**

```markdown
## rootcause reflection

### symptom
invoice totals display incorrect values (off by cents)

### 5 whys tree

#### branch 1: calculation path
1. why is the total wrong? → line items sum doesn't match displayed total
2. why doesn't the sum match? → round applied per-line instead of at end
3. why is round per-line? → `formatCurrency()` called on each line item
4. why was formatCurrency used? → copy-pasted from UI display code
5. why was it copy-pasted? → **ROOT CAUSE**: no dedicated `computeInvoiceTotal` operation

#### branch 2: data path (ruled out)
1. why is the total wrong? → could be data issue?
2. checked: data correct at source → **NOT ROOT CAUSE**

### root cause summary

| layer | issue | is root cause? |
|-------|-------|---------------|
| display | shows wrong total | no (symptom) |
| calculation | rounds per-line | no (symptom) |
| architecture | absent domain operation | **YES** |

### recommendation
address at architecture layer: introduce `computeInvoiceTotal` domain operation.
```

**3.1.5.research.reflection.product.premortem._.v1.i1.md:**

```markdown
## premortem reflection

### imagine we shipped and it failed — why?

1. **we broke other calculations**: the fix changed formatCurrency behavior globally
2. **we missed edge cases**: negative amounts, refunds, multi-currency
3. **we didn't test with real data**: test data didn't have the precision issues
4. **finance team wasn't consulted**: they had additional requirements we missed

### what assumptions could turn out wrong?

| assumption | what if wrong? |
|------------|----------------|
| formatCurrency is the only source of round errors | other formatters may exist |
| all invoices use same round rules | some invoice types may differ |
| fix is backwards compatible | old invoices may display differently |

### how would a critic attack this?

- "you only fixed one symptom — what about quotes? estimates? receipts?"
- "you didn't add tests for the edge cases you listed"
- "you changed shared code without check of all callers"

### nightmare scenario

we deploy the fix, finance team approves, then quarterly audit reveals some historical invoices now display differently than archived copies. compliance investigation ensues.

### mitigations to consider

1. audit all callers of formatCurrency before change
2. add explicit tests for negative, refund, multi-currency
3. ensure historical invoice display is frozen/versioned
4. get finance team sign-off before deploy
```

## mental model

### how would users describe this to a friend?

> "before we build, we stop and ask three questions: who cares? why really? what could go wrong? it's like therapy for code — you have to understand the deeper issues before you can fix them."

### what analogies or metaphors fit?

| reflection | analogy |
|------------|---------|
| audience | doctor asks "where does it hurt?" before they prescribe |
| rootcause | detective asks "why?" until they find the real culprit |
| premortem | pilot runs through failure scenarios before takeoff |

the triad forms a **reflection checkpoint** — like a pilot's pre-flight check but for behavior design.

### what terms would they use vs what terms would we use?

| user term | our term | sense |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| "who's this for?" | audience reflection | empathy |
| "why really?" | rootcause reflection | depth |
| "what could go wrong?" | premortem reflection | caution |
| "the actual fix" | root cause fix | fix at source |
| "thought it through" | reflection stones | deliberate pause |

## evaluation

### how well does it solve the goals?

| goal | how solved |
|------|------------|
| prevent bandaid fixes | rootcause reflection + self-review |
| ensure user empathy | audience reflection |
| surface risks early | premortem reflection |
| document rationale | reflection artifacts preserve the work |
| enable review | reviewer sees explicit analysis |

### what are the pros?

- **empathy**: audience reflection ensures we build for real users
- **durability**: rootcause fixes don't regress
- **caution**: premortem surfaces risks before they bite
- **documentation**: reflections become part of behavior record
- **confidence**: wisher sees the work was thought through

### what are the cons?

- **overhead**: adds three research stones to behaviors
- **skill required**: good reflections take practice
- **false negatives**: self-review might pass weak reflections
- **scope creep risk**: reflections might expand scope beyond wish

### what edgecases exist and how do our contracts keep users in a pit of success?

| edgecase | mitigation |
|----------|------------|
| audience is "everyone" | force specificity: primary, secondary, tertiary |
| root cause is out of scope | justified symptom treatment with follow-up tracked |
| premortem finds showstopper | good — better to know now than after ship |
| reflections feel redundant for simple fix | allow brief "N/A — obvious" with justification |

## open questions & assumptions

### what assumptions have we made?

1. **three reflections are sufficient**: audience, rootcause, premortem cover the key blind spots
2. **reflection order matters**: audience first, then rootcause, then premortem
3. **self-review can validate**: assumes the has-addressed-reflections check works

### what questions remain unanswered?

1. should reflection stones be required or optional? **ask wisher**
2. should all three always run, or can mechanic pick relevant ones? **ask wisher**

## what is awkward?

### what feels off or forced?

- **three stones might feel heavy** for simple fixes
- **"reflection" is new category** — may need explanation
- **premortem overlaps** with rootcause (both ask "why?") — need clear distinction

### where does the design fight the user's mental model?

- mechanics might want to "just fix it" without three reflection exercises
- "premortem" is unfamiliar term for some
- to distinguish audience vs rootcause vs premortem takes practice

### what tradeoffs feel uncomfortable?

- thoroughness vs speed: three stones add time
- flexibility vs enforcement: required vs optional debate
- depth vs pragmatism: how deep is deep enough?
